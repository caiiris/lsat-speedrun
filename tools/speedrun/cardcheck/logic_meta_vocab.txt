## §1. Argument Structure Vocabulary

§1.1 argument
An argument is a set of statements in which one statement (the conclusion) is claimed
to follow from, or be supported by, the remaining statements (the premises). Not every
collection of sentences is an argument; an argument requires at least one premise and
exactly one main conclusion.

§1.2 premise
A premise is a statement offered as evidence, reason, or support for the conclusion.
Premises are the reasons given for accepting the conclusion. An argument can have one
or more premises.

§1.3 conclusion
The conclusion is the claim that the premises are intended to establish or support.
It is what the arguer is trying to convince you of. An argument has exactly one main
conclusion.

§1.4 inference
An inference is the cognitive process of deriving a conclusion from a set of premises.
In formal usage, "inference" also refers to a statement derived from other statements
via a rule of reasoning. Inference is the move from premises to conclusion.

§1.5 assumption
An assumption is an unstated premise — a claim that the argument requires in order for
the conclusion to follow, but which the argument does not explicitly state. Identifying
assumptions is central to evaluating argument strength.

§1.6 necessary assumption
A necessary assumption is one without which the argument's conclusion cannot hold. If
the negation (denial) of the assumption destroys the argument, the assumption is
necessary. The negation test: if denying the claim defeats the conclusion, it is a
necessary assumption.

§1.7 sufficient assumption
A sufficient assumption is one that, if added as a premise, guarantees (is enough to
establish) the conclusion on its own. A sufficient assumption closes the gap between
the premises and the conclusion.

§1.8 deductive validity
A deductive argument is valid if and only if it is impossible for all its premises to
be true and its conclusion to be false. In a valid argument the truth of the premises
guarantees the truth of the conclusion. Validity concerns logical form, not actual truth.

§1.9 soundness
A deductive argument is sound if and only if it is both valid and all of its premises
are actually true. Soundness implies truth of the conclusion.

§1.10 inductive strength
An inductive argument is strong if the premises make the conclusion probable (likely
true). Unlike validity, inductive strength admits of degrees. A cogent inductive
argument is both strong and has actually true premises.

§1.11 enthymeme
An enthymeme is an argument with one or more unstated (suppressed) premises or a
suppressed conclusion. The missing piece is assumed to be understood from context.

§1.12 counterexample
A counterexample is a specific case or instance that disproves a universal claim. If
someone claims "All A are B," one A that is not B is a counterexample that falsifies
the claim.

§1.13 logical equivalence
Two statements are logically equivalent if they have the same truth value in all
possible situations — whenever one is true, the other is also true, and whenever one is
false, the other is also false. Logically equivalent statements can substitute for each
other in an argument without changing its validity.

§1.14 conditional statement
A conditional statement has the form "If P then Q" (written P → Q). P is the
antecedent (sufficient condition); Q is the consequent (necessary condition). The
conditional asserts only that if P is true, Q must be true — not that P is true.

§1.15 contrapositive
The contrapositive of "If P then Q" is "If not-Q then not-P" (¬Q → ¬P). The
contrapositive is logically equivalent to the original conditional. Forming the
contrapositive is the standard technique for reasoning from a denied consequent to a
denied antecedent.

## §2. Indicator Words

§2.1 conclusion indicators
Words and phrases that signal the conclusion: therefore, thus, hence, consequently,
so, it follows that, shows that, demonstrates that, which means that, we can conclude
that, this proves that, accordingly.

§2.2 premise indicators
Words and phrases that signal a premise: because, since, for, given that, as indicated
by, the reason is that, on account of, assuming that, in light of the fact that, as
shown by, owing to.

§2.3 "therefore"
The word "therefore" is a conclusion indicator. The statement that follows "therefore"
is the conclusion of the argument. The statement before "therefore" is typically a
premise.

§2.4 "since"
The word "since" (used in a logical, non-temporal sense) is a premise indicator. The
statement that follows "since" is a premise or reason supporting the claim made
elsewhere in the sentence.

§2.5 "because"
The word "because" is a premise indicator. What follows "because" gives the reason or
evidence for the claim made in the main clause.

§2.6 "it follows that"
The phrase "it follows that" is a conclusion indicator. The statement following "it
follows that" is the conclusion drawn from the preceding premises.

§2.7 "given that"
The phrase "given that" is a premise indicator. It introduces a premise or assumption
being granted for the sake of the argument.

§2.8 "which shows that"
The phrase "which shows that" is a conclusion indicator. The observation or evidence
stated earlier supports the claim introduced by "which shows that."

## §3. Quantifiers and Quantifier Reasoning

§3.1 universal quantifier "all"
The word "all" (universal quantifier) asserts that every member of the subject class
has the predicate property. "All A are B" means: for every individual x, if x is an A
then x is a B. A single counterexample (an A that is not B) falsifies the statement.

§3.2 existential quantifier "some"
The word "some" (existential quantifier) asserts that at least one member of the
subject class has the predicate property. "Some A are B" is true as long as there
exists at least one thing that is both A and B.

§3.3 negation of "All A are B"
The negation of "All A are B" is "Some A are not B." If it is not the case that every
A is a B, then there must be at least one A that lacks the property B.

§3.4 negation of "No A are B"
The negation of "No A are B" is "Some A are B." If it is not the case that no A is a
B, then there must be at least one thing that is both A and B.

§3.5 "most"
The word "most" asserts that more than half (>50%) of the subject class has the
predicate property. "Most A are B" is stronger than "Some A are B" (which requires
only one) but weaker than "All A are B" (which requires every one).

§3.6 sufficient condition
In a conditional "If A then B," A is the sufficient condition. A's truth is enough to
guarantee B's truth. However, A is not the only possible way to bring about B.

§3.7 necessary condition
In a conditional "If A then B," B is the necessary condition. B must be true if A is
true; without B, A cannot be true. The falsity of B (not-B) guarantees the falsity of
A (not-A) — the contrapositive.

## §4. The Flaw (Fallacy) Catalog

§4.1 ad hominem
The ad hominem fallacy attacks the person making the argument rather than the argument
itself. Even if the person has flaws, those flaws do not automatically make the
argument wrong. The argument must be evaluated on its own merits.

§4.2 straw man
The straw man fallacy misrepresents someone's argument — typically exaggerating or
distorting it — in order to make it easier to attack. The arguer refutes the
misrepresented version rather than the actual argument.

§4.3 false dilemma (false dichotomy)
The false dilemma presents only two options as if they were exhaustive when in fact
other options exist. "Either X or Y; not X; therefore Y" is only valid if X and Y
really are the only possibilities.

§4.4 circular reasoning (begging the question)
Circular reasoning uses the conclusion as a premise — either explicitly or in
disguised form — so that the argument assumes what it is trying to prove. No
independent evidence is offered for the conclusion.

§4.5 correlation-causation fallacy
The correlation-causation fallacy assumes that because two events or variables are
correlated (occur together or covary), one causes the other. Correlation is evidence
for causation but does not establish it; alternative explanations (reverse causation,
common cause, coincidence) must be ruled out.

§4.6 sufficient-necessary confusion
The sufficient-necessary confusion treats a sufficient condition as if it were a
necessary condition, or a necessary condition as if it were sufficient. If "studying
guarantees a pass" (sufficient), erroneously concluding "not studying guarantees
failure" commits this fallacy (the inverse error). Similarly, confusing the
converse with the conditional is a form of this error.

§4.7 equivocation
The equivocation fallacy uses the same word or phrase in two different senses within
an argument. The argument appears valid only because the shift in meaning is concealed.

§4.8 composition fallacy
The composition fallacy (part-to-whole) assumes that what is true of the individual
parts of something must also be true of the whole. Parts having a property does not
entail the whole has that property.

§4.9 division fallacy
The division fallacy (whole-to-part) assumes that what is true of the whole must also
be true of each of its parts. A whole having a property does not entail that each
component has that property.

§4.10 slippery slope
The slippery slope fallacy claims that a relatively small first step will, without
adequate justification, inevitably lead to a chain of events culminating in some
significant and usually negative consequence. The fallacy lies in the unwarranted
assumption of the chain.

§4.11 appeal to authority (ad verecundiam)
The appeal to authority accepts a claim solely or primarily because an authority figure
asserts it, without independent support. This is fallacious when the authority is not
relevant to the subject, or when legitimate experts disagree.

§4.12 appeal to popularity (ad populum)
The appeal to popularity (bandwagon) accepts a claim because many people believe it.
The number of believers does not determine the truth of a claim.

§4.13 hasty generalization
The hasty generalization draws a broad conclusion from a sample that is too small or
not representative of the relevant population.

§4.14 red herring
The red herring introduces information that is irrelevant to the conclusion in order
to distract from the real issue. The arguer diverts attention by raising a tangential
point.

§4.15 post hoc ergo propter hoc (false cause)
Post hoc ergo propter hoc ("after this, therefore because of this") assumes that
because event B followed event A in time, A caused B. Temporal succession is not
sufficient evidence for causation.

§4.16 appeal to ignorance (ad ignorantiam)
The appeal to ignorance claims that a proposition is true because it has not been
proven false, or false because it has not been proven true. The absence of evidence
is not by itself evidence of absence.

§4.17 scope error (overgeneralization)
A scope error applies a rule, generalization, or statistic to a case that falls
outside the domain for which it was established, or treats a qualified claim as if it
were universal.

§4.18 sampling bias
Sampling bias occurs when the sample used to draw a conclusion is not representative
of the target population, systematically over- or under-representing some subgroup.
Conclusions drawn from a biased sample may not hold for the population at large.

§4.19 is-ought gap (naturalistic fallacy)
The is-ought gap (Hume's guillotine) is the error of inferring a normative conclusion
("ought") purely from a descriptive premise ("is"). What is the case does not by
itself determine what ought to be the case.

§4.20 affirming the consequent
Affirming the consequent is an invalid argument form: "If P then Q; Q is true;
therefore P is true." Q being true does not guarantee P, because Q might have other
causes. This is the converse error.

§4.21 denying the antecedent
Denying the antecedent is an invalid argument form: "If P then Q; P is false;
therefore Q is false." P's falsity does not rule out Q, because Q might follow from
something else. This is the inverse error.
